General Information

Location-Speakers

Beja is a closely related group of language varieties and dialects spoken in the Red Sea coastal and hill regions in Sudan, Eritrea, and Egypt by more than a million individuals. The variety documented here is that of the Arteiga tribe in the vicinity of Port Sudan and Suakin, Sudan. This variety was studied by Richard Hudson (see especially Hudson 1976), and the data has been made conveniently available in a survey article by David Appleyard (Appleyard, 2007a).

PNG Properties

  • Arteiga Beja makes more PNG distinctions than are found in the Cushitic-Omotic PNG Pattern

    Although Beja has the same property-value features as the rest of Cushitic (viz., sg/pl, p1/p2/p3, m/f), it has a more extensive (and more archaic) set of gender distinctions:

    number person gender
    sg p1 "⊤"
    sg p2 "m"
    sg p2 "f"
    sg p3 m
    sg p3 m
    sg p3 f
    pl p1 "⊤"
    pl p2 "m"
    pl p2 "f"
    pl p3 "m"
    pl p3 "f"

    I.e., where gender is distinguished not only in the third-person singular, but also in the third-person plural, and in the second-person singular and plural,.

Verb Properties

In the Beja verb generally, there is a complex interaction among the categories of polarity (with a marked affirmative-negative asymetry), conjugation class, root class, tense stem class, and tense-aspect-mode marking. The following tables to give a general orientation. For detail, the actual paradigms must be inspected and compared.

For all verb classes, tense-aspect-mode morphology is built on a system of seven stems. For the three root classes of the prefixing verb the stems are differentiated by affixation and internal vowel change; the suffixing conjugation stem remains invariable, but as can be seen from the paradigms themselves, comparable vowel alternations and affix series can be detected in the inflectional suffixes (which are commonly assumed to originate from encliticized stem-changing auxuliaries). Note that in the negative stem forms, even the suffixing conjugation verb receives prefixes.
